# Crypto Rise And Fall, 2010-2024

## Thesis

Crypto rises when a new asset form creates believable upside, transferable ownership, and enough liquidity for strangers to coordinate. Crypto falls when leverage, custody, insider supply, regulatory force, or narrative exhaustion reveals that the upside was funded by later entrants.

The long arc has two stable survivors: self-custodied scarce assets and infrastructure that earns real usage. Most other waves are experiments in turning belief into liquidity.

## Master Cycle

1. **Discovery**: a new primitive appears and early users gain asymmetric upside.
2. **Narrative compression**: complex technology becomes a simple slogan.
3. **Liquidity ignition**: exchanges, AMMs, airdrops, listings, or memes convert attention into tradable flow.
4. **Insider distribution**: teams, miners, VCs, KOLs, market makers, or early holders sell risk to later entrants.
5. **Reflexive growth**: price validates story, story attracts buyers, buyers support price.
6. **Fragility accumulation**: leverage, emissions, debt, unlocked supply, and weak custody accumulate.
7. **Break point**: withdrawal freeze, peg break, exploit, unlock, regulation, volume collapse, or narrative rotation.
8. **Survivor sorting**: durable assets keep users, weak projects become screenshots, legal cases, and folklore.

## Era Narrative

### 2010-2012: The Frontier

The first era was curiosity plus operational chaos. Bitcoin was small enough for technically curious users to mine, trade, lose keys, arbitrage, and learn market structure firsthand. The edge came from being early, understanding custody, tolerating primitive tooling, and surviving mistakes.

The fall pattern was also born here: high-yield deposits, early leverage venues, exchange hacks, and informal trust networks. BTCST shows the core template: fixed high returns attract deposits, market stress triggers withdrawal pressure, and the system breaks when promised returns outrun liquid backing.

Historical lesson: early markets reward hands-on skill, then punish users who treat trust as infrastructure.

### 2013-2014: Industrialization And Custody Crisis

ASICs turned Bitcoin from hobby mining into industrial competition. Capex, electricity, supply chains, chip design, and regional policy entered the asset's price structure. Mining created forced sellers and forced believers at the same time.

MtGox exposed the central paradox: Bitcoin can be decentralized while the market trades through centralized choke points. A protocol can keep producing blocks while the dominant exchange freezes withdrawals, invents technical explanations, carries fake balances, and destroys user trust.

Historical lesson: protocol risk and venue risk need separate ledgers.

### 2015-2016: Winter As Filter

After the first mania, the market selected for people who still cared about nodes, wallets, mining, security, and monetary theory. The quiet period gave long-term holders a language: private keys, full nodes, cost basis, ahr999-style valuation, and "币本位" thinking.

The fall pattern was boredom. Weak hands left, builders continued, and the next wave was seeded by infrastructure rather than headlines.

Historical lesson: bear markets build the next cycle's rails and reputations.

### 2017: ICO And Civil War

The 2017 cycle had two engines: ICO fundraising and Bitcoin governance conflict.

ICO changed startup finance by making early-stage claims liquid from the beginning. The good version lowered fundraising barriers and made token ownership auditable. The bad version sold whitepapers, celebrity trust, exchange listings, and future dreams to public buyers with weak rights.

The blocksize war showed that hashpower, domains, exchanges, and loud narratives could challenge BTC's identity, while full nodes, users, developers, and legitimacy defended it. The lesson was social: a blockchain's security includes the people who refuse hostile redefinition.

Historical lesson: public liquidity before product accelerates both innovation and fraud.

### 2018-2019: Aftermath, Mode Coins, And Peg Dreams

The ICO winter revealed dead projects, trapped retail, and token models with weak reason to exist. The market then simplified speculation into mode coins: capital, CX-style distribution, concept packaging, and active market making.

Algorithmic stablecoins showed another recurring failure: a system can promise reflexive stabilization, then fail when debt has no real payer. When a coin falls below peg, rescuers ask who funds the rescue and who captures upside. If bond buyers only hold an unenforceable promise, the peg defense becomes theater.

Historical lesson: when product disappears and mode remains, the game becomes exit timing.

### 2020: DeFi Turns The Casino Into Software

DeFi summer created transparent, composable, global financial machinery. AMMs, lending pools, governance tokens, liquidity mining, and yield aggregators proved that open contracts can coordinate huge amounts of capital.

The fall pattern was equally structural. Native-token emissions paid yield, yield attracted TVL, TVL justified token price, token price funded yield. Exploits, admin powers, oracle issues, front-end attacks, and composability contagion made "audit passed" a narrow claim.

Historical lesson: a yield system is only as strong as its payer, weakest dependency, and exit queue.

### 2021: Culture, Institutions, And State Power

NFTs turned digital culture into financial inventory. PFPs, collectible status, fractionalization, and marketplace liquidity made JPEGs trade like altcoins with cultural metadata. This created real communities and extreme illiquidity traps.

Mining shutdown in China showed that state power can hit geography while hashpower can migrate. El Salvador showed that sovereign adoption can create legitimacy and geopolitical exposure together. The cycle expanded crypto from code and finance into culture and politics.

Historical lesson: culture can create price floors, then liquidity determines whether those floors are tradable.

### 2022: Leverage Unwinds

The 2022 collapse repeated older lessons at institutional size. Reflexive stablecoins, lenders, funds, and exchanges relied on circular collateral, hidden leverage, and confidence. When prices fell, balance sheets became stories asking for belief.

The old MtGox lesson returned: withdrawal rights matter more than account screenshots. The old stablecoin lesson returned: debt needs a payer. The old DeFi lesson returned: composability transmits failure.

Historical lesson: in a crisis, the only assets that count are withdrawable, liquid, and controlled by enforceable keys or credible institutions.

### 2023-2024: Points, Restaking, L2s, And Meme Factories

The market shifted toward delayed promises and fast attention. Points postponed token valuation. Restaking stacked yield and slashing assumptions. L2s and modular infra sold future fee markets. Solana-style meme factories compressed launch, attention, liquidity, and exit into short windows.

The three-plate model explains the era well: staking/restaking as dividend plates, AMM/casino/meme pools as pooling plates, L1 sub-assets and meme launchpads as splitting plates. Survivors are systems that keep users, developers, and liquidity after incentives fade.

Historical lesson: when the market sells "future airdrop", treat the promise like debt with uncertain conversion.

## Recurring Rise Drivers

- New ownership primitive: BTC, token, NFT, LP share, point, restaked claim.
- New liquidity venue: exchange, AMM, launchpad, marketplace, bridge, perpetuals.
- New narrative wrapper: digital gold, world computer, fair launch, metaverse, modular, AI, anti-VC, meme.
- New participant class: miners, ICO buyers, yield farmers, NFT collectors, institutions, airdrop farmers, meme traders.
- New distribution machine: KOLs, Telegram, Discord, X, exchanges, quests, points.

## Recurring Fall Triggers

- Withdrawal freeze or reserve doubt.
- Peg break or failed redemption.
- Native-token yield death spiral.
- Insider unlocks and market-maker inventory.
- Contract exploit, admin abuse, oracle/bridge failure.
- Regulatory shutdown or banking chokepoint.
- Narrative rotation and volume collapse.
- Users discover that "community" meant locked exit liquidity.

## Survivability Test

Ask:

1. Can the asset survive a 70% price drawdown?
2. Can users exit without destroying the system?
3. Can the project pay rewards from non-reflexive revenue?
4. Can the system operate after incentives end?
5. Can insiders sell without becoming the whole demand story?
6. Can the project explain its value without price charts?
7. Can users verify reserves, contracts, and permissions directly?
8. Can the asset keep cultural or monetary demand through boredom?

## Final Operating Claim

The history of crypto is a repeated conversion of belief into liquid markets. The best projects convert belief into durable networks. The worst projects convert belief into insider exits.
